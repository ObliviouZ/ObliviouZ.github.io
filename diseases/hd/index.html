 Huntington's Disease 

Introduction

Huntington disease (HD) is an inherited condition that causes progressive degeneration of neurons in the brain. Signs and symptoms usually develop between ages 35 to 44 years and may include uncontrolled movements, loss of intellectual abilities, and various emotional and psychiatric problems. People with HD usually live for about 15 to 20 years after the condition begins. It is caused by changes (mutations) in the HTT gene and is inherited in an autosomal dominant manner. This means that having a change (mutation) in only one of the 2 copies of the HTT gene is enough to cause the condition. When a person with HD has children, each child has a 50% (1 in 2) chance to inherit the mutated gene and develop the condition.

Treatment is based on the symptoms present in each person and may include various medications.

First Described

The first recorded mention of this disease was made in 1842, in a letter written by Charles Oscar Waters and published in Practice of Medicine. In 1846, Charles Gorman noted that the disease seemed to occur in particular regions. In 1860, Johan Christian Lund also gave a description of the disease, in which he noted that dementia along with jerking movement disorders seemed to be particularly prevalent in a secluded area of Setesdalen in Norway.

In 1872, George Huntington gave the first complete description of the disease based on his studies of several generations of one family who exhibited similar symptoms. Huntington outlined the pattern of autosomal dominant inheritance years before Mendelian inheritance was elucidated.

Epidemiology

According to systematic reviews and meta-analytic studies, prevalence of HD worldwide is between 2.5 and 3 per 100.000 [1][1],[2][2].

Causative gene

The IT15 gene, also known as huntingtin gene, is affected in Huntington's disease. It has a total of 67 exons and is located in the short arm of chromosome 4 (4p16.3, cytogenic band) (figure 3). There is an increased ammount of CAG repeats (CAG expansion) in exon 1 in Huntington's patients, usually more than 39 repeats, but less or more repeats have other implications already documented and demonstrated in (figure 2) [4][4]-[6][6] . This gene codes for the protein huntingtin (Htt).

Codified protein: structure, domains, functions and intracellular localization

Structure and domains

Huntingtin (Htt or HTT) is a soluble 3144 aminoacid protein (~350 kDa), found mostly expressed in the Central Nervous System (CNS) and testes. The abnormal polyglutamine expansion in the N-terminal region of Htt produces significant dysfunction and neural death [7][7].

The N-terminal Gln-rich and Pro-rich domain has great conformational flexibility and is likely to exist in a fluctuating equilibrium of alpha-helical, random coil, and extended conformations. Protein functions and biological role

Htt has various important cellular and biological roles:

  • Essential for embryonic development, as Htt gene knockout has been shown to be lethal in mice by embryonic day 8.5 [8][8].
  • Increased cellular death in Htt knockout animals indicate an anti-apoptotic role for Htt, which is supported by the findings that overexpression of full-length wild-type Htt in cultured striatal neurons protects them from apoptosis, whereas depletion of wild-type Htt makes neurons more sensitive to apoptotic stimulus[9][9] - [11][11].
  • Htt can bind and prevent the formation of the pro-apoptotic Hip1-HIPPI (Hip1 protein interactor) complex [12][12].
  • Htt can directly block the activation caspase 3 and 9 [9][9], [13] [13].
  • Htt regulates the production of the brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) [14][14], [15] [13].
  • Htt acts as positive regulator of BDNF transcription by preventing the recruitment of REST/NRSF to its DNA response element [16][16], [17] [17].
  • By interacting with a variety of endocytic/trafficking proteins, such as α-adaptin, HIP1, HIP14, HAP1, HAP40, PACSIN1 and SH3GL3, clathrin, and dynamin, Htt participates in long and short range axonal transport and vesicle trafficking by associating with vesicles and microtubules [18][18] , [19][19], stimulates BDNF vesicular trafficking in neurons [20][20] , [21][21], regulates synaptic activity in neurons and regulates synaptic transmission and plasticity [22] [22].

Protein intracellular localization

Immunofluorescent studies with cells in culture have supported both a cytoplasmic and nuclear localisation of HTT [23][23] , [24][24].

Many initial studies have supported Htt as an exclusively cytoplasmic protein [25][25] , [26][26] and others have shown nuclear localization of Htt [27][27] , [28][28].

The current idea of Htt intracellular localization is that it is largely cytoplasmic, but it can also be found with the cell nucleus[24][24] .

Pathophysiology

Huntington’s disease neuropathological mechanisms are documented and mostly surround proteolytic cleavage of mHTT, misfolding, aggregation, transcriptional problems/dysregulation, mitochondrial dysfunction and abnormal cell clearance of HTT (Ubiquitin-proteasome system and autophagy).

Mutant HTT proteolysis results in N-terminal fragments where the polyglutamine tract is in HTT. These fragments are more prone to aggregate and cause neuronal toxicity. It is believed that mHTT aggregation and further cleavage by activating proteolytic caspases are directly involved with cell death [29][29] . Protease inhibitors, or mutations that may block HTT cleavage have been demonstrated to slow down HD progression in mice [30][30] , [31][31].

When it comes to misfolding and aggregation of mHTT the topic becomes less clear. Inclusions are often seen in the cell nucleus but also form in the cytoplasm, dendrites and axon terminals, though to a lesser extent. HTT fragments aggregate depending on the size of polyglutamine, fragment size, and intracellular protein interactors [32][32] - [35][35] . Inclusions are heterogeneous population comprising different forms of mHTT, and their definitive role (whether toxic or protective) has not yet been established [36][36] .

Transcriptional problems that arise from HD are usually related with altered function/activity of several transcription factors and cofactors that HTT interacts with. Mutant HTT (with expanded polyglutamine) has been reported to bind and change the activity of several transcription factors and other effectors [37][37] , [38][38] . When mHTT binds these transcriptional factors/cofactors it sequesters these away from their normal DNA elements. Normal Htt binds cytosolic REST/NRSF and normally prevents its translocation to the nucleus, whereas mHTT interacts less with REST/ NRSF, resulting in the accumulation of this factor in the nucleus [16][16] , [17][17] . Mutated HTT also occupies the promoter region of PGC-1α by associating with the CREB–TAF4 complex. This leads to suppression of PGC-1α which in instance leads to evidence on impaired metabolic energy observed in HD [39][39] . HTT could also impact mitochondrial biogenesis and function through effects on PGC-1α gene expression which was mentioned above as a target for mHTT.

Mutant HTT has been reported to damage mitochondria by multiple mechanisms: reduced energy production and membrane potential, alteration in calcium homeostasis, more oxidative stress, and triggering of the caspase-mediated apoptotic pathways [40][40] - [42][42] .

About UPS interference. HTT with expanded polyglutamine sequesters UPS components into HTT inclusions altering their subcellular localization and consequently decaying the UPS efficiency [43][43] , [44][44] . Another method of interfering with the UPS consists on polyglutamine HTT not being degraded efficiently within the proteasome, leading to expanded polyglutamine sequences trapped in the proteasome that may block the entry of other substrates [45][45] . It has also been proposed that inefficient degradation of expanded polyglutamine tracts of proteins may lead to the production of aggregation-prone fragments [46][46] .

Autophagy may also be slightly compromised in some cases. Mutant HTT can interfere with target recognition and impair autophagic clearance [47][47] , [48][48] . Two articles have presented (in animal models) autophagy as a protecting mechanism capable of clearing the cell of mutated forms of huntingtin [49][49] , [50][50] .

Clinical manifestations: neuropathology, other signs and symptoms

Neuropathology

Neuropathologically, HD is primarily characterized by neuronal loss in the striatum, cortex and basal ganglia. Nuclei usually affected are: globus pallidus (GP), thalamus, hypothalamus, subthalamic nucleus, substantia nigra (SN) and cerebellum [51][51] .

Diffusion tensor imaging has demonstrated pathology of the white matter in pre and early symptomatic patients [52][52] .

Signs and symptoms

Huntington disease (HD) is a progressive disorder that causes motor, cognitive, and psychiatric signs and symptoms. On average, most people begin developing features of HD between ages 35 and 44. Signs and symptoms vary by stage and may include:

Early stage
  • Behavioral disturbances
  • Clumsiness
  • Moodiness
  • Irritability
  • Paranoia
  • Apathy
  • Anxiety
  • Hallucinations
  • Abnormal eye movements
  • Depression
  • Impaired ability to detect odors
Middle stage
  • Dystonia
  • Involuntary movements
  • Trouble with balance and walking
  • Chorea with twisting and writhing motions
  • Unsteady gait
  • Slow reaction time
  • General weakness
  • Weight loss
  • Speech difficulties
  • Stubbornness
Late stage
  • Rigidity
  • Bradykinesia
  • Severe chorea
  • Serious weight loss
  • Inability to speak
  • Inability to walk
  • Swallowing problems
  • Inability to care for oneself

 Spinocerebellar Ataxia 6

Introduction

Spinocerebellar ataxia type 6 (SCA6) is one of the autosomal dominant cerebellar ataxias (ADCAs), and its main clinical feature is slowly progressive ataxia consistent with ADCA type III, namely, pure cerebellar ataxia. SCA6 is caused by a defect in a gene that makes a subunit of a voltage gated channel, involved in several processes, including calcium ion import. The genetic defect in this protein results in degeneration of primarily Purkinje cells of the cerebellum.

SCA6 is an autosomal dominant disease, which means that individuals of either sex are equally likely to inherit the gene and develop the disease, and that passes directly from one generation to the next without skipping generations. Each child of a person with SCA6 (heterozygous) has a 50 percent chance of inheriting the gene that causes SCA6.

First Described

In 1997, an article correlated SCA6 with a polymorphic CAG repeat in the human α1A voltage-dependent calcium channel subunit. It was the first time Spinocerebellar ataxia type 6 has been reported to be associated with triplet repeats in the CACNA1A gene [1][1].

Epidemiology

The frequency of SCA6 varies considerably according to geographical area. SCA6 represents only about 2 percent of the cases of dominant spinocerebellar ataxia in Italy, 31 percent in Japan, 1%-2% in Spain and France, 3% in China, 12% in the US and 13% in Germany [2][2] (Figure 1).

According to National Ataxia Foundation, in the United States, SCA6 represents about 15 percent of all cases of dominant hereditary ataxia. Overall, the prevalence of this disease is estimated at less than 1 in 100,000.

Spinocerebellar ataxia type 6 (SCA6) estimated worldwide prevalence is less than 1/100,000, according to Orphanet.

Causative gene

The gene affected in this disease is the CACNA1A. It has a size of 300kb with a minus strand orientation. This gene has 47 exons, according to many sources and the pathological CAG repeats are found in exon 47 (Figure 2). It is in chromosome 19 (cytogenic band: 19p13.13) and the gene translates to a calcium gated channel and another protein translated via cap-independent manner currently denominated as a1ACT (Figure 3).

Normal length of the CAG repeat tract at the C-terminus of the a1A subunit of the P/Q-type voltage-gated calcium channel CACNA1A ranges between 4 and 18 glutamines. In SCA6 the tract is expanded to between 19 and 33 glutamines.

Codified protein: structure, domains, functions and intracellular localization

Structure and domains

CACNA1A gene codes for the voltage-dependent P/Q-type calcium channel subunit α1A. This channel has a length of 2506 aa and a mass of 282 564 Da (Isoform 1). This channel is mostly expressed in the brain (neuronal tissue) followed by the stomach and is involved in a variety of calcium-dependent processes.

It is now known that the C-terminus of the a1A subunit (a1ACT) is physiologically formed from the mRNA of the full-length subunit [3][3] and translocated to the nucleus and is directly involved in gene expression regulation.

Protein functions and biological role

Voltage-gated Ca2+ channels (VGCC) are of extreme importance because influx of Ca2+ through these channels regulate various cellular processes, including neurotransmission and gene expression [4][4]:

  • Involved in synaptic transmission and integration [5][5].
  • Involved in gene regulation [6][6].
  • Plays a crucial role in membrane excitability, triggering synaptic transmission and neuronal plasticity [7][7].

The C-terminus of the α1A subunit (α1ACT) is the one more directly involved in gene expression and therefore the α1A subunit is often given the gene expression regulation function. This fragment is translated in a way termed: cap-independent, which occurs when the 40S ribosome directly binds mRNA near the start codon at an internal ribosome entry site (IRES) [8][8].Through an AT-rich element of α1ACT binding site, α1ACT is capable of:

  • Binding genes expressed in the neurons.
  • Regulating gene expression in neurons.

As a transcription factor, the normal α1ACT enhances the expression of at least three genes, GRN, PMCA2 and BTG1, in PC12 cells and cerebellar tissue [9][9].

Protein intracellular localization

According to Protein Atlas and Uniprot, this channel protein is mainly localized in cell membranes. Within neurons it can be found in the nucleus, plasma membrane (in the voltage-gated calcium channel complex) and other locations such as cell projections, cytoplasm and synapses.

Pathophysiology

The involvement of a functional ion channel subunit (α1A) of a voltage-gate calcium channel in SCA6 favours the hypothesis that SCA6 is an ion channel disorder in which the polyglutamine expansion in the C-terminus of the α1A subunit causes ataxia by altering calcium channel function. This theory had conflicting results and later had some evidence against it [10][10], [11][11].

When it came to the C-terminus of the α1A subunit (α1ACT), there was an article on HEK cell cultures which presented that expressing C-termini containing Q28 were significantly more toxic than C-termini harbouring Q13 [12][12]. This toxicity is independent of the function of the full-length protein. In addition, like other polyglutamine diseases the toxicity is highly dependent on the nuclear localization of the expanded protein.

The α1ACT with polyglutamine expansion is found within cerebellar tissue and localizes to Purkinje cell nuclei, and that nuclear localization is not affected by polyglutamine length [13][13].

Compared to the properties of the normal α1ACT protein, the α1ACT with expanded glutamines has altered binding to various regular targets affecting normal cell functioning [9][9].

Formation of insoluble aggregates, or more direct interference by soluble forms of the mutant protein, with certain vital components of cellular metabolism or nuclear processes has been invoked as pathogenic mechanisms, but remains to be determined whether the accumulation of aggregates contributes to the pathogenesis or protection from the disease [14][14].

Clinical manifestations: neuropathology, other signs and symptoms

Neuropathology

The most common neuropathologic findings in individuals with SCA6 consist on either selective Purkinje cell degeneration or a combined degeneration of Purkinje cells and granule cells [15][15]. Morphological changes in the few remaining Purkinje cells can be seen [15][15], [16][16], such as heterotopic nuclei, unclear cytoplasmic outline, somatic sprouts, dendritic swelling with increased numbers of spine-like protrusions and disorganization of axonal arrangements. In contrast to other SCAs, where cell loss and gliosis in additional regions can be seen secondary to the profound loss of Purkinje cells, very limited degeneration is detected in associated cortical or cerebello-olivary regions in SCA6 [15][15], [17][17]. Some decrease in the number of neurons in the inferior olivary nuclei may be detected when disease duration is long [18][18].

Signs and symptoms

Spinocerebellar ataxia type 6 (SCA6) is characterized by adult-onset, slowly progressive cerebellar ataxia. The age at which symptoms first occur varies widely, from age 19 to 71, but is typically between 43 and 52.

Inital symptoms
  • Gait unsteadiness
  • Stumbling
  • Imbalance
  • Dysarthria
As disease progresses
  • Gait Ataxia
  • Upper-limb incoordination
  • Intention tremor
Common symptoms
  • Dysphagia
  • Choking
  • Oculomotor disorders
  • Peripheral neuropathy
